For this project, I collected 33 days worth of data, collecting 6 variables (date, time, reading completed in minutes, reading completed in pages, sleep duration and sleep quality) once per day. I recorded the number of minutes and pages read each day because it gave me two different data types to work with for each reading session, and I could also use them to calculate pages read per minute in case reading efficiency was a better indicator for sleep quality. Number of minutes slept was recorded to test for an alternative impact to sleep quality, and then sleep quality was recorded based on how I felt waking up to test trends with the other data collected. My original data mostly had sleep quality values ranging between 2 to 3.5, so these values were scaled so that 2 became 1, 2.5 became 2, etc., to fit the original five-star scale. So ultimately, a sleep quality score of 1 was when I struggled to get out of bed due to either a headache or fatigue, 2 was when I struggled to get out of bed without any physical impairment, 3 was when I could get out of bed without struggling, 4 was when I was when I woke up well-rested and eager to start the day, and 5 would have been if I woke up excited felt physically better than average.

After reviewing my visualization, I have noticed a positive correlation between bedtime reading and sleep quality. A majority of my days without reading scored below a 3 for sleep quality (10 out of 11), while a majority of my reading days scored 3 or above (17 out of 22). Using the sort filters, I can also see some other correlations, such as longer sleep durations more consistently recording lower sleep quality scores, and also mostly being days without reading. However, shorter sleep durations seem to correlate with better sleep quality scores, and are more consistently nights where bedtime reading occurred. The greater amount of pages read also seems to have some correlation with improved sleep quality scores compared to lower reading amounts. In conclusion, my hypothesis seems to be supported by my data collection and could potentially find a stronger correlation between bedtime reading and sleep duration with more data in the future.
